# Will Arinze

## Meeting Details

- **Date:** Jun 23
- **Participants:** Brom Sulaiman, Mike, William Arinze
- **Meeting title:** Will Arinze <> Oxford Growth catchup

## Overview

Will shared how his networking needs have evolved as his business has matured. Early on, networking was mainly about understanding the Oxford ecosystem and building awareness of the local landscape. More recently, it has become a way to build strategic relationships with people and organisations he may work with, learn from, or integrate with in future.

He responded positively to the Oxford Growth concept, especially the idea of bringing together local business leaders who are beyond the earliest startup stage and want trusted, non-salesy peer relationships. A recurring theme in the conversation was the difficulty of finding people who are one or two stages ahead, especially in a local context where there is enough trust and regular contact to make advice genuinely useful.

## Why Will Networks

- Initially, to understand the Oxford ecosystem, organisations, and support structures.
- Later, to identify people he could potentially partner with or work with.
- Now, less about selling and more about strategic awareness.
- He wants to stay close to interesting people, ideas, and adjacent opportunities he may want to adopt in the future.
- He values being in rooms where he is not the most advanced person, so he can continue learning.

## Key Insights For Oxford Growth

### 1. Trusted advice is valuable

Will's strongest signal was that it is hard to find people who are slightly ahead of you and relevant to your current stage. A valuable network would help members know who to turn to for trusted, practical advice.

### 2. Locality matters

He sees real value in groups that are local enough for people to meet regularly and build trust, even if "local" means the broader Oxfordshire area rather than one postcode.

### 3. The group should avoid direct selling

He responded well to the idea that the community is about helping one another grow rather than pitching services at each other. This was contrasted with some existing networking experiences where people lead with what they are selling.

### 4. Stage matters

He distinguished between startup-focused communities and something more useful for leaders who already have traction, revenue, or teams. He felt this positioning was different from what BIPC currently offers.

### 5. Regularity matters, but not necessarily monthly

He highlighted that some of the most useful groups are regular enough to build momentum, but not so frequent that attendance becomes unrealistic. He mentioned that quarterly-style rhythms can feel about right.

### 6. Relationship quality drives attendance

He noted that he still prioritises some events because of the relationship with the organiser and the support they have previously provided. This suggests community stickiness may depend as much on trust and curation as on format.

## Observations On Existing Networks

### BIPC

- He was the first member of the BIPC and has seen it evolve from launch to its current state.
- He believes BIPC works well because it is accessible and easy to drop into.
- He sees it as broad and startup-friendly, but less tailored for more established leaders.
- He expects changes following Ryan's departure, with some energy likely transferring elsewhere.

### Other groups he mentioned

- **NHS cohort accelerator**: more accelerator-focused and not directly comparable.
- **OUI**: part of an earlier partnership experience.
- **Blenheim Palace leaders / leaders in industry**: senior, high-level, paid access, not necessarily tech-specific.
- **NX Members / leaders in tech**: aimed at younger leaders, around four times a year, with a cadence he described as sensible.

## Format And Commercial Model Considerations

Will surfaced several useful design questions:

- Should the group be open drop-in or more curated?
- How often should members meet: monthly, bi-monthly, quarterly, or less often?
- Should the experience be primarily in-person, digital, or a mix of both?
- Should there be a free model, a paid model, or a freemium tier structure?
- How exclusive should the group be in terms of role, stage, or revenue?

He also pointed out that pricing changes the kind of room you create. He gave the example of high-ticket Oxford events where the cost buys access to very senior decision-makers.

## Signals Of Value

What seemed most appealing to Will was:

- learning from people one or two steps ahead;
- being able to help others without the interaction feeling transactional;
- meeting peers who face similar leadership and growth challenges;
- having access to people who can either solve a problem or point you to someone who can.

## Potential Risks Or Things To Avoid

- Building something too broad, where the audience is unclear.
- Creating a community that feels like a sales channel rather than a peer network.
- Running events too frequently for busy operators to attend consistently.
- Making the format so rigid that people cannot drop in when useful.

## Suggested People To Speak To

- **Rhys Hathaway**: runs Hathaway's lettings / estate agency business in Oxford; Will suggested he could be a strong person to speak with.
- **Louise Chandler**: marketing-focused, well connected across Oxfordshire, works with larger organisations, and was described as knowledgeable without being pushy.

## Notable Quotes / Themes

- Networking has shifted for him from awareness-building to strategic relationship-building.
- He wants to avoid being "the best in the room" and continue learning from people further ahead.
- The most useful community would help members know where to turn for trusted advice.

## Takeaways For Us

- The proposition appears strongest if positioned around trusted peer support for leaders with traction.
- A locally rooted but selectively curated network feels more differentiated than another broad startup community.
- Cadence, trust, and quality of relationships may matter more than scale.
- The anti-sales positioning resonated clearly and should likely remain central.

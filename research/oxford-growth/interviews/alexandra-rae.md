# Alexandra Rae

## Meeting Details

- **Date:** Jun 30
- **Participants:** Brom Sulaiman, Alexandra Rae, Mike
- **Meeting title:** 30 min with Brom (Alexandra Rae)

## Overview

Alexandra shared the perspective of a long-standing Oxfordshire business leader running an established wealth management firm with a strong relationship-led model. Her firm has grown primarily through trust, referrals, and long-term client relationships, with newer marketing activity layered on more recently. That background shapes how she thinks about networking: less as direct selling and more as a way to build visibility, credibility, and meaningful connections over time.

Her feedback suggested that Oxfordshire already has a wide range of networking formats, but that each serves a slightly different need. She highlighted the importance of being very clear about target audience, format, and value proposition before launching anything new. She also pointed to a possible opportunity around lower-friction virtual formats, topic-led workshops, and small trusted groups where leaders can discuss strategic issues more openly than they would in broader social networking settings.

## Why Alexandra Networks

- To raise awareness of Wise Investment beyond existing client referrals.
- To build relationships with other professionals, introducers, and local business leaders.
- To support long-term business development through trust and repeated visibility rather than immediate selling.
- To stay connected to Oxfordshire's business community across different types of groups and formats.
- To access both social community and more strategic peer discussion, depending on the format.

## Key Insights For Oxford Growth

### 1. Clarity of positioning is essential

Alexandra's strongest signal was that there are already many networking groups in and around Oxfordshire, each with a distinct format, audience, and tone. For Oxford Growth to stand out, it will need a very clear answer to who it is for, what problem it solves, and how it differs from existing options.

### 2. Different formats serve different needs

She described structured referral-style groups, large established business communities, informal social lunches, and more curated executive circles. This suggests Oxford Growth should be deliberate about whether it wants to be a networking group, a peer learning space, a strategic discussion forum, or a blend of several formats.

### 3. Trusted small-group discussion may be the real gap

While Alexandra does not feel personally underserved by the current market, she did point to the value of smaller, trusted groups where leaders can discuss strategic questions, operational issues, and sensitive topics. That kind of environment requires careful curation and psychological safety, especially if people are discussing real business challenges.

### 4. Virtual networking may be an overlooked opportunity

She noted that she has seen relatively little high-quality virtual networking since Covid, despite the convenience it offers. This could be a useful route for Oxford Growth, particularly for busy leaders who would not commit to travel-heavy in-person events.

### 5. Delivery matters more than promise

She gave a clear example of how her view of a network improved when it delivered tangible value and weakened when that value became less consistent. Advocacy will depend on whether Oxford Growth reliably does what it says it will do.

## Observations On Existing Networks

### Open Doors

- Structured and formal, with meetings every two weeks.
- Less sales-heavy than BNI, but still process-driven and attendance-based.
- Became too burdensome for her over time because of the regular commitment.

### BNI

- She sees it as overly sales-focused and too pushy for her taste.
- Weekly attendance was immediately unattractive.

### The Cotswolds

- Female-only and more community-led than traditional networking.
- Has grown quickly and appears to have strong local momentum and brand pull.
- Feels more social and lifestyle-oriented than formal business networking.

### Bizpedia

- Positioned as a more high-end networking offer with good venues and a polished format.
- Combines monthly meetings with quarterly events such as golf days.
- Appears focused on a certain calibre of business audience.

### B4

- Established network with larger companies and a wider set of formats.
- Offers quarterly flagship events plus newer curated formats such as the CEO Circle.
- Comes closest to strategic peer discussion, especially in smaller executive groups.

### Co Club

- Informal lunches or dinners in strong Cotswold venues.
- More relationship-led and conversational than structured networking.

### Oxford Business Community Network

- James Craddock's network, which she said has grown significantly after a format change.
- Sounds more like a larger open event environment than a curated peer group.

## Format And Commercial Model Considerations

Alexandra surfaced several useful design considerations:

- Be explicit about whether the group is for general networking, strategic discussion, peer support, or sector-specific connection.
- Consider whether the audience is small businesses, larger SMEs, or more established leaders.
- A highly structured format can work, but may become burdensome for busy operators.
- Small trusted groups are better suited to strategic or sensitive topics than large open events.
- Virtual formats may be underused and could reduce time friction for attendees.
- Topic-led workshops or speaker sessions could be more productive than generic mingling alone.
- In-person events should justify the time commitment, since many current formats effectively take up half a day.
- If the aim is also to create business for the founders, she cautioned that running a network well can itself become a full-time job.

## Signals Of Value

What seemed most valuable to Alexandra was:

- building long-term trust and visibility with the right people;
- joining formats that fit the time and energy she has available;
- having a mix of social connection and commercially relevant relationship-building;
- accessing useful discussion on real leadership and business topics;
- participating in something that is thoughtfully curated and easy to understand.

## Potential Risks Or Things To Avoid

- Launching without a clear enough distinction from existing networks.
- Building something too broad, where the intended audience is unclear.
- Over-indexing on in-person events that are too time-consuming.
- Expecting a networking community to generate business quickly without major ongoing effort.
- Creating spaces where people are uncomfortable sharing because trust has not been established.
- Letting the experience drift away from the value originally promised.

## Suggested People To Speak To

- **James Craddock**: runs Oxford Business Community Network, is well connected locally, and may have useful perspective on format and demand.
- Alexandra also offered to make introductions to other business owners, particularly if given more detail on the types of members Oxford Growth most wants to reach.

## Notable Quotes / Themes

- Oxfordshire already has many networking offers, so any new one needs a very clear reason to exist.
- The most interesting whitespace may be virtual, lower-friction, or small-group strategic formats rather than another generic event series.
- Good networking is not necessarily sales-focused, but it often creates commercial outcomes over time through trust and consistency.

## Takeaways For Us

- Differentiation needs to be sharper than "another Oxfordshire network".
- There may be stronger opportunity in strategic peer support, topic-led discussion, or virtual matchmaking than in large in-person events.
- Small trusted groups appear better suited to commercially sensitive conversations than open networking rooms.
- A hybrid model could be worth testing: light-touch online connection plus more curated small-group sessions.
- The proposition will need to be simple to explain and reliable in execution if it is going to earn referrals.

## Overview

Poppy shared the perspective of a senior operator coming out of an intensive scale-up journey in life sciences. After joining Arctoris as one of the first employees outside the founding team and eventually becoming COO, she is now at a transition point in her career and beginning to explore networking more deliberately beyond the science sector.

Her feedback was especially useful because it came from someone who has spent years in a highly specialised ecosystem and is now actively testing broader business communities. The strongest themes were that cross-industry exposure feels energising and valuable, structured conversations are easier than unstructured mingling, and a trusted network is often more useful for solving specific problems than the abstract idea of “getting a mentor”.

## Why Poppy Networks

- To build a broader network beyond science, suppliers, clients, and sector conferences.
- To meet people from very different industries and learn from how they approach similar leadership challenges.
- To find energising, cross-disciplinary conversations rather than staying inside one professional bubble.
- To create access to practical help, introductions, and problem-specific advice during a career transition.
- To understand what opportunities and communities exist in Oxfordshire outside her previous operating context.

## Key Insights For Oxford Growth

### 1. Cross-industry diversity is a major source of value

Poppy was clear that one of the most useful parts of networking is exposure to people with very different backgrounds, industries, and geographies. She finds those conversations intellectually stimulating and often transferable, even when the context is not directly related to her own work.

### 2. Structured formats help people engage more easily

She noted that events are easier to navigate when there is some structure, such as a talk, a theme, or guided discussion, rather than simply putting people in a room and telling them to network. That structure lowers the initial awkwardness and makes better-quality conversations more likely.

### 3. Smaller groups may create deeper connection than large rooms

Although she has not attended many roundtable-style sessions herself, she was positive about the idea of smaller, moderated discussions focused on a topic or problem. Her instinct was that those environments are more likely to create meaningful relationships than larger open networking rooms.

### 4. A directory can be more useful than generic mentoring rhetoric

One of her most practical points was that people often say you should “get a mentor”, but very few people explain how to do that well. A directory or network that helps members find targeted advice on a specific issue may be more useful than a vague mentoring proposition.

### 5. Hybrid access matters, but in-person still has the edge

Poppy said in-person interaction generally feels more valuable, but she also saw a strong case for online formats when designed well, especially if they use small breakout groups. That suggests accessibility matters, but the design has to work harder online to avoid passive participation.

## Observations On Existing Networks

### Women in Business

- She recently attended Women in Business events and found them useful because they exposed her to a much broader group of people than she would meet in science.
- The variety of backgrounds felt energising and gave her a sense of what else exists in the Oxfordshire ecosystem.

### MBA cohort

- Her Oxford Brookes MBA has been a strong source of cross-industry learning.
- She highlighted the value of hearing from people in entirely different sectors and then adapting their lessons to her own context.

### Science and sector-specific networking

- Her previous experience was mostly limited to clients, suppliers, and scientific conferences.
- She felt that this can create a narrow professional world where networking remains highly field-specific.

### Milton Park fika and subgroup model

- She pointed to Milton Park’s themed `fika` sessions as a useful example of light structure around a topic with coffee, pastries, and networking.
- She also highlighted the way those larger sessions appeared to spawn more specific subgroups such as HR and site operations circles.

## Format And Commercial Model Considerations

Poppy surfaced several useful design considerations:

- Use some structure, such as a talk, theme, or prompt, rather than relying on pure mingling.
- Consider smaller moderated roundtables for deeper discussion and better-quality connection.
- Mix times of day rather than assuming everything should happen after work.
- Recognise that accessibility and caring responsibilities may affect who can attend when.
- In-person events may generate stronger outcomes, but online can still work if designed around small breakout discussion.
- Topic-led sessions may help people self-select into the discussions most relevant to them.
- A directory with lightweight profile tags could help people signal what they can help with and what they are looking for.
- Themed, repeatable formats such as the Milton Park `fika` model may provide a useful pattern to test.

## Signals Of Value

What seemed most valuable to Poppy was:

- learning from people in very different industries and roles;
- having discussion formats that reduce awkwardness and encourage better conversation;
- making deeper connections rather than just meeting lots of people briefly;
- finding targeted advice on a real, current problem;
- being part of a trusted network where members can offer practical help to one another.

## Potential Risks Or Things To Avoid

- Over-relying on completely unstructured networking with no prompt or facilitation.
- Assuming post-work timing works equally well for everyone.
- Designing online sessions that allow a few voices to dominate while others disengage.
- Making the mentoring proposition too vague rather than helping people solve specific problems.
- Letting group quality drift over time if the right people stop engaging.

## Suggested People To Speak To

- **Sally Cullen**: Poppy’s former colleague and Chief People Officer at Arctoris; she was described as thoughtful, experienced across both small and large organisations, and likely to have a useful perspective.

## Notable Quotes / Themes

- Diversity of background can be more energising and useful than staying within one sector.
- Good networking often feels serendipitous, but the format can still make better conversations more likely.
- People may not need a mentor in the abstract so much as access to someone who has solved a very specific problem before.

## Takeaways For Us

- Cross-industry breadth appears to be a genuine strength rather than a compromise.
- Structured formats may outperform “here are some drinks, go network” events.
- A smaller-group or roundtable model could be worth testing early.
- A member directory with areas-of-help signals could become a meaningful product feature.
- Mixing in-person depth with well-designed online accessibility may widen participation without losing too much quality.
- Themed sessions could be a practical bridge between broad networking and more focused peer support.

# Mike Pytharidis

## Meeting Details

- **Date:** Jun 26, 2026
- **Participants:** Brom Sulaiman, Nathalie Sparrow, Mike Pytharidis
- **Meeting title:** Oxford Growth Introduction- Nathalie/Brom & Mike

## Overview

Mike shared a broad commercial and operational background spanning marketplaces, SaaS, energy, home improvement, product, engineering, sales, and people operations. A strong theme throughout the conversation was that he sees himself as a generalist leader, which has become more valuable as he has moved into more senior roles and can connect problems across the whole business lifecycle.

His feedback on Oxford Growth was encouraging and specific. He sees a gap in Oxfordshire for a more senior, better-curated leadership network that helps people build relevant relationships, find trusted peer support, and access growth opportunities beyond their day job. He was particularly clear that the value will depend on the right level of matching between people, the right density and frequency of interaction, and a clear separation from hard-selling behaviour.

## Why Mike Networks

- To connect with other senior leaders in the area beyond his immediate role.
- To find opportunities for mentoring, advisory work, and broader professional growth.
- To build relationships that may become useful over time rather than for immediate transactional gain.
- To support others and learn through helping them solve problems.
- To access peers who are at a similar or slightly more advanced stage in their leadership journey.

## Key Insights For Oxford Growth

### 1. Seniority and relevance matter more than volume

Mike described poor networking experiences as those where the room is full of junior attendees and sponsors trying to sell into them. He responded much more positively to curated groups made up of peers with enough experience and decision-making responsibility to have valuable conversations with one another.

### 2. The right matchmaking is essential

One of his clearest points was that not all senior people are useful to one another in the same way. He emphasised the need to match people by stage, context, and challenge so that the group is not just senior in title, but genuinely relevant in practice.

### 3. Oxfordshire lacks density compared with London

He sees London as easier for networking because of the density of people and the frequency of events. In Oxfordshire, he believes the lower density means the format needs to be more intentional, more localised, and less frequent.

### 4. A community should create long-tail value

He is not looking for immediate sales opportunities. What he wants is a network where something useful might emerge in three months, six months, or later, whether that is advice, mentoring, introductions, or an advisory opportunity.

### 5. Anti-sales positioning is important

He drew a clear distinction between representing yourself as a leader with a broader agenda and showing up simply to generate leads. In his view, aggressive selling would quickly damage the quality of the network.

## Observations On Existing Networks

### CIPD events

- He helped run CIPD events early in his career.
- He saw a repeated mismatch between junior attendees seeking to learn and sponsors seeking to sell.
- A more valuable format emerged when HR director-level groups were kept separate and discussion was shaped around the people in the room.

### Oxfordshire / OxLEP-style support

- He referenced Oxfordshire-focused support bodies as feeling too broad, basic, or "vanilla".
- He found some of the content useful for newer founders, but not especially valuable for more seasoned operators.
- He sees this as part of the gap Oxford Growth may be able to fill.

### Enterprise Nation

- He mentioned Enterprise Nation as a potentially relevant comparison point with a broad professional pool.
- He had a positive experience when it delivered on its promise to connect him with a company to mentor.
- His enthusiasm dropped when the value became less consistent over time.

### London event ecosystems

- He cited companies such as Twilio, Aircall, Revolut, and Stripe as examples of organisations creating regular, high-density networking opportunities.
- He sees London as stronger because events happen frequently and attract enough people to make attendance feel worthwhile.

## Format And Commercial Model Considerations

Mike surfaced several practical design considerations for how the network should work:

- He would prefer something no more frequent than every six weeks.
- A bi-monthly or quarterly cadence feels more realistic than monthly.
- Travel should ideally be limited to around 30 minutes for in-person attendance.
- Access to trains and convenient locations matters, especially around Oxford.
- Oxfordshire may need multiple smaller regional gatherings rather than one county-wide event if travel becomes a barrier.
- Smaller curated groups are more appealing than large open networking events.
- Topic-based gatherings could work well if the right people are selected for each theme.

## Signals Of Value

What seemed most valuable to Mike was:

- being around relevant senior professionals rather than generic networking crowds;
- finding people who can offer advice tied to his next stage of growth;
- discovering advisory, mentoring, or non-executive opportunities over time;
- helping others and learning through those interactions;
- attending something that clearly delivers on the promise it makes.

## Potential Risks Or Things To Avoid

- Allowing people to attend purely to pitch their services.
- Building a room that is too junior to create meaningful peer exchange.
- Hosting events too frequently for busy senior operators.
- Creating groups that are too broad and lack clear segmentation.
- Running large events where meaningful conversation is difficult.
- Failing to deliver consistently on the value proposition once members join.

## Suggested People To Speak To

- Mike did not name specific people during the call, but suggested targeting people who have recently stepped into head of, director, or exec-level roles.
- He offered to suggest contacts later if sent more detail on the types of members Oxford Growth is looking for.

## Notable Quotes / Themes

- Generalist leaders can become especially valuable at senior levels because they can see how decisions in one part of the business create problems elsewhere.
- Oxfordshire networking currently feels too vanilla for more experienced operators.
- The most useful network would help people build relationships that lead to mentoring, advice, or opportunities over time rather than immediate sales.

## Takeaways For Us

- The proposition looks strongest when positioned for experienced leaders rather than broad startup audiences.
- Curation and segmentation are likely more important than scale.
- Small, relevant groups may outperform big general events.
- The network should be explicit that it is not a lead-generation environment.
- Cadence should probably be bi-monthly or quarterly rather than monthly.
- Delivering consistently on the value promise will be critical to advocacy and retention.

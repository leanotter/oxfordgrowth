## Meeting Details

## Snapshot

Amir shared a pragmatic private equity and M&A perspective shaped by multiple exits and hands-on work with both investors and growth-stage businesses raising capital. His view was that there is a genuine market for specialist product and technology diligence, particularly below the top end of the market where buyers want strong external input without paying tier-one advisory fees.

The clearest theme was that credibility and network matter more than a polished website or generic positioning. He encouraged a route into private equity through adjacent M&A work, paired with a broad but well-structured offer that can flex between pre-acquisition diligence, fundraise support, and post-acquisition strategic advice depending on the situation.

## Who They Are / Relevant Context

- Former operator with multiple private equity exits and direct experience of both deal processes and post-deal operating realities.
- Now works with private equity firms and companies that want to raise money, combining advisory, deal support, and strategic input.
- Brings a perspective shaped by fundraising, M&A, portfolio support, and transaction credibility rather than purely design or product work.
- Particularly useful as a source on how specialist advisory offers get bought, trusted, and positioned in PE-adjacent markets.

## Key Commercial Insights

### 1. There is a market for product-focused diligence

Amir was clear that there is demand for external support on product, commercial, and technical diligence, especially among acquirers and growth businesses that want a specialist view without defaulting to the largest advisory firms. The need appears particularly strong in the lower and mid-market, where buyers still need confidence but may not want six-figure diligence spend.

### 2. M&A is a practical route into private equity work

Rather than trying to start with private equity firms directly, he suggested entering through mergers and acquisitions work with companies already buying other businesses. That creates a more accessible route to proving value, building credentials, and eventually becoming known to PE-backed and PE-adjacent buyers.

### 3. Impartiality is part of the value proposition

He reinforced that external advisers are useful because they can provide a separate, candid assessment that internal teams often do not have the time, space, or objectivity to produce themselves. That aligns well with specialist product reviews where someone independent can identify hidden risks, weak retention dynamics, or strategy gaps.

### 4. Positioning should be broad enough to flex by buyer need

Amir cautioned against becoming too narrow too early. His own framing spans deal preparation, fundraise support, and strategic advice, allowing him to adapt the offer to what a client actually needs rather than leading with a single rigid specialism.

### 5. Reputation drives access more than marketing polish

His strongest commercial advice was that this market runs on relationships, referrals, and repeated exposure. A credible website matters as basic proof, but being visible in the right rooms, speaking to enough people, and becoming recommended by trusted contacts matters far more.

## How This Market Buys

- Work is bought largely through relationships, referrals, and repeated exposure rather than cold brand-led demand.
- Buyers often want an external, impartial perspective rather than relying solely on internal teams.
- The exact buyer can vary: private equity firms, acquisitive growth businesses, founders preparing for a deal, or portfolio leaders needing support.
- A credible website helps establish legitimacy, but it is not the main driver of demand.
- Hard-selling is likely to be counterproductive; buyers respond better when the value becomes obvious through conversation and recommendation.

## Where Our Offer Fits

- Pre-acquisition: strongest fit around product, technical, and commercial diligence before a deal completes.
- Post-acquisition: clear fit around churn, retention, product strategy, and growth improvement work in portfolio companies.
- Deal prep: useful where founder-led businesses need help identifying the issues that could reduce price or create diligence concerns.
- Advisory / retainer: viable as an ongoing model for businesses that want candid external support beyond a single project.
- Adjacent M&A support: likely a practical entry point before trying to sell directly into PE firms.

## Diligence / Value Creation Priorities

- Buyers want to know whether they are acquiring something fundamentally sound.
- Product and technical risk can materially affect price, confidence, and deal appetite.
- Hidden dependencies, weak operational depth, and concentration of knowledge in one person are major red flags.
- External assessment is valuable when it helps surface the issues that could chip valuation or create post-deal problems.
- Value creation work becomes more credible when linked to measurable areas such as retention, growth, scalability, and execution risk.

## Positioning And Messaging Implications

- Lead with a simple core proposition, then expand based on the prospect's situation.
- Frame the work in terms of how it helps the buyer, rather than leading with capabilities for their own sake.
- Avoid sounding like a generalist agency trying to sell into PE.
- Be careful not to over-specialise so early that the market becomes too narrow.
- A broader anchor such as digital platforms and product strategy may be more useful than over-indexing on a single vertical.

## GTM / Relationship Strategy

- Build access through M&A and acquisitive growth businesses as well as PE-adjacent contacts.
- Spend time with the actual target audience rather than relying on passive marketing.
- Speaking, referrals, and direct relationship-building appear to be stronger channels than outbound brand marketing alone.
- Be explicit about who the offer is for, since different audiences will respond to different angles.
- There may be strategic value in separating PE-facing positioning from broader market positioning.

## Commercial Model Considerations

- Project-based diligence looks credible, especially around pre-acquisition assessment.
- Retainer-based advisory work also appears viable and may become a meaningful revenue stream.
- The offer can span diligence, fundraise support, and strategic advice if packaged carefully.
- Separate branding may help if one audience is pure private equity and another is broader founder or growth-business work.
- The commercial model should leave room for refinement rather than being locked too early.

## Risks / Challenges

- Positioning the offer too narrowly and limiting the market unnecessarily.
- Positioning it too broadly and making the differentiation unclear.
- Approaching private equity firms with an obvious hard-sell posture.
- Relying too heavily on a website instead of relationship-building.
- Keeping the offer static instead of refining it as market feedback comes in.
- Underestimating how much credibility and referrals matter in this space.

## Suggested People To Speak To

- No specific names were suggested in the conversation.
- Amir did suggest spending time in the right M&A and private equity circles and becoming clearer on the exact target audience before expanding outreach.

## Takeaways For Us

- A product and technology diligence offer appears commercially credible, especially in the lower and mid-market.
- Entering through M&A support for acquisitive businesses may be easier than trying to sell directly into PE from day one.
- The proposition should likely anchor around digital product and platform expertise, while staying broad enough to flex by client need.
- Relationship-led business development will matter more than marketing assets alone.
- There may be value in treating PE and non-PE audiences as distinct go-to-market motions, or even separate brands.
